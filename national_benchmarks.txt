### (1) Short Summary (2–3 sentences)

For **sleep**, large U.S. surveys around 2015–2018 suggest adults overall report about **7.5–7.7 hours of sleep per night on average**, and 2020 NHIS data show that roughly **one-quarter to one-third of adults (including women 18–44)** sleep under 7 hours; however, no 2020+ national mean is published specifically for **women 30–39**. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][1])

For **moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA), daily steps, and resting heart rate (RHR)**, U.S. public-health surveillance since 2020 mainly reports guideline adherence and broad “normal ranges” rather than precise age-specific means; **no reliable 2020+ national mean values specific to adults 30–39 (or women 30–39 for RHR) could be identified**. Older NHANES accelerometer and pulse data (1999–2008, 2003–2006, 2005–2006) provide background but are now dated and not directly comparable to modern devices or post-COVID behavior. ([PubMed][2])

### (2) Detailed Findings (Bullet Lists)

#### Indicator 1 — Average Sleep Duration

> “Average nightly sleep duration of women aged 30–39 in the United States.”

* **Age range used**

  * Best available *mean* sleep-duration data: **U.S. adults ≥18 years**, NHANES 2015–2018 (no published 30–39-specific mean in 2020+ data located). ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][1])
  * Sleep difficulties and short-sleep prevalence: **adults ≥18**, with subgroup reporting for **18–44 years** by sex in NHIS/CDC briefs. ([cdc.gov][3])

* **Mean value**

  * NHANES 2015–2018 analysis of U.S. adults reports a **total average sleep duration of about 7.6 hours per night (7.65 ± 1.58 h)**. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][1])
  * This estimate is for **all adults**, not specifically women 30–39. Age-specific means for women 30–39 could not be located in publicly accessible 2020+ national datasets.

* **Country/region**

  * **United States**, nationally representative samples (NHANES, NHIS). ([jamanetwork.com][4])

* **Year(s) of data collection**

  * Mean sleep duration: **2015–2018** (NHANES). ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][1])
  * Sleep difficulties and short sleep prevalence: **2020 NHIS** (sleep difficulties) and **2013–2014 or 2017–2019 NHIS/BRFSS** (short sleep <7 h by age and sex). ([cdc.gov][3])

* **Key descriptive findings (2020+ relevant)**

  * 2020 NHIS: >25% of U.S. adults do **not** meet the ≥7-hour recommendation; sleep difficulties (trouble falling or staying asleep, daytime tiredness) are common. ([cdc.gov][3])
  * MMWR QuickStats show that among adults **18–44 years**, roughly **30% of women** and **28–30% of men** report sleeping <7 hours on average, indicating that **most but not all** young adults meet the 7-hour threshold. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][5])

* **Source name(s)**

  * **National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) 2015–2018** – U.S. adults, self-reported sleep duration. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][1])
  * **National Health Interview Survey (NHIS) 2020** – NCHS Data Brief “Sleep Difficulties in Adults: United States, 2020”. ([cdc.gov][3])
  * **MMWR QuickStats** – Percentage of adults who sleep <7 hours, by sex and age group. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][5])

* **Methodological / limitation notes**

  * All cited sleep measures are **self-reported hours in a typical 24-hour period**, rounded to the nearest hour in NHIS; this can introduce rounding and recall bias. ([jamanetwork.com][4])
  * NHANES 2015–2018 average (≈7.6 h) is **pre-2020** and may not fully reflect post-pandemic sleep changes.
  * No public 2020+ U.S. national tabulation was found that reports **mean hours specifically for women aged 30–39**. Any application to that subgroup requires strong caution and should be treated as **background only**, not a precise benchmark.

#### Indicator 2 — Moderate-to-Vigorous Physical Activity (MVPA)

> “Average daily moderate-to-vigorous physical activity minutes for adults aged 30–39.”

* **Age range used**

  * U.S. guidelines: **adults 18–64 years** (Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2nd ed.). ([health.gov][6])
  * Surveillance of guideline adherence typically covers **adults ≥18** and does *not* publish nationally representative mean MVPA minutes per day by single age band like 30–39 for 2020+. ([www.heart.org][7])

* **Mean value**

  * **No reliable 2020+ national estimate of “average daily MVPA minutes” for adults aged 30–39 in the U.S. is available.**
  * Public-health reports instead emphasize **proportion meeting guidelines**, not mean daily minutes.

* **Guideline context (not a measured mean)**

  * U.S. and WHO recommendations: at least **150–300 minutes/week of moderate-intensity** or **75–150 minutes/week of vigorous-intensity** aerobic activity (or an equivalent combination) for adults 18–64 — i.e., roughly **21–43 minutes of MVPA per day** as a *target*, not an observed average. ([health.gov][6])
  * AHA 2024 statistical update notes that only **~24.2% of U.S. adults** meet both aerobic and muscle-strengthening recommendations, implying that **typical population MVPA is below guideline targets**, but it does not provide a mean daily MVPA duration. ([www.heart.org][7])

* **Country/region**

  * United States, national surveillance (NHIS, NHANES) informing federal guidelines and AHA statistics. ([health.gov][6])

* **Year(s) of data collection (for key references)**

  * Physical Activity Guidelines 2nd ed.: evidence base largely up to **2018**, still used in 2020s policy. ([health.gov][6])
  * AHA Heart Disease and Stroke Statistics: 2024 update summarizing latest national surveys. ([www.heart.org][7])

* **Pre-2020 background (non-benchmark)**

  * Accelerometer-based analyses using **NHANES 2003–2006** describe low volumes of MVPA, but results are highly sensitive to device type and MVPA cut-points and are **not reported as simple national means for 30–39-year-olds**. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][8])
  * Because these data are more than a decade old and methods have evolved, they are **unsuitable as a precise 2020+ benchmark** for today’s 30–39 age group.

* **Source name(s)**

  * **Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2nd edition** (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services). ([health.gov][6])
  * **2024 Heart Disease and Stroke Statistics – AHA** (summary of U.S. physical activity adherence). ([www.heart.org][7])
  * **NHANES accelerometry analyses 2003–2006** (e.g., Strath et al., Wolff-Hughes et al.), used only for historical context. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][8])

* **Methodological / limitation notes**

  * MVPA estimates depend strongly on **how accelerometer counts per minute are translated into “moderate” or “vigorous”** thresholds, and whether activity must be in ≥10-minute bouts. ([ResearchGate][9])
  * Post-2020, consumer wearables and smartphones have become more common, but **their data are not yet integrated into a standardized, nationally representative 30–39-specific MVPA benchmark**.
  * Because of these issues, providing a single numeric “average daily MVPA minutes” for adults 30–39 in the U.S. would require speculation, which is **not ethically acceptable** here.

#### Indicator 3 — Daily Steps

> “Average daily step count of adults aged 30–39.”

* **Age range used**

  * Best-known U.S. step-count data come from **NHANES 2005–2006** in adults **≥20 years**; age-specific means for 30–39 are not easily extractable from publicly accessible summaries. ([PubMed][2])
  * More recent summaries (2024–2025) often report **average American adults** taking about **4,000–5,000 steps per day**, aggregating multiple international cohorts and U.S. samples, but typically **not broken out by exact age band 30–39**. ([Verywell Health][10])

* **Mean value**

  * **No reliable 2020+ U.S. national estimate of “average daily step count” specifically for adults aged 30–39 is available.**
  * Historical background (not a 2020+ benchmark):

    * NHANES 2005–2006 accelerometer study of U.S. adults ≥20 years reported mean **~9,700 steps/day including all steps** and **~6,500 steps/day when very-low-intensity steps were censored**, across the adult population. ([PubMed][2])

* **Country/region**

  * United States (NHANES), plus international cohorts summarized in recent dose–response meta-analyses on steps and health outcomes. ([PubMed][2])

* **Year(s) of data collection (for key references)**

  * NHANES accelerometer steps: primarily **2005–2006**. ([PubMed][2])
  * International meta-analyses on daily steps and mortality: datasets up to about **2021–2022**; umbrella review updated through **2024–2025**. ([thelancet.com][11])

* **Pre-2020 background (non-benchmark)**

  * The NHANES 2005–2006 step-count data are based on **hip-worn accelerometers**, while today’s counts often come from **wrist-worn wearables or smartphones**, which systematically differ in step detection. ([cdc.gov][12])
  * As a result, these older values **cannot be straightforwardly used as a precise “current” benchmark** for 30–39-year-olds in the 2020s.

* **Source name(s)**

  * **“Accelerometer-determined steps per day in U.S. adults”** (NHANES 2005–2006 analysis). ([PubMed][2])
  * **Lancet Public Health / Lancet Public Health umbrella/meta-analyses** of steps and mortality (Paluch et al. 2022; Ding et al. 2025). ([thelancet.com][11])
  * **Consumer-facing summaries** (Verywell Health, etc.) that synthesize multiple cohorts but are not, themselves, primary data sources. ([Verywell Health][10])

* **Methodological / limitation notes**

  * Devices, placement (hip vs wrist vs phone), and algorithms for step detection have **changed substantially** over time, making direct comparison of 2005–2006 NHANES counts with 2020s wearable data problematic. ([cdc.gov][12])
  * Many recent, large step-count studies are **international and not U.S.-exclusive**, so their “average” values represent mixed populations. ([thelancet.com][11])
  * Given these constraints, assigning a single U.S. “average daily step count” for adults 30–39 in 2020+ would be **speculative** and is therefore avoided.

#### Indicator 4 — Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

> “Average resting heart rate of women aged 30–39 in the United States.”

* **Age range used**

  * Clinical and public-health references typically provide **broad adult ranges (≥10 or ≥18 years)**, not narrow national estimates for women 30–39. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][13])
  * Historical NHANES 1999–2008 “resting pulse rate reference data” include age- and sex-specific distributions, but detailed means for women 30–39 are not readily extractable via open summaries. ([ResearchGate][14])

* **Mean value**

  * **No reliable 2020+ U.S. national estimate of “average resting heart rate” specific to women aged 30–39 was identified.**
  * Contemporary U.S. and international clinical references consistently describe a **“normal” adult resting range of about 60–100 beats per minute (bpm)**, with lower values common in more physically fit individuals. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][13])
  * Within this range, population distributions often show many adults clustering in the **60s–70s bpm**, but precise U.S. 30–39-specific means are not available from 2020+ national datasets accessible here.

* **Country/region**

  * United States for NHANES-based reference data, plus global/US-oriented clinical guidance (AHA, major reviews). ([ResearchGate][14])

* **Year(s) of data collection (for key references)**

  * NHANES resting pulse reference: **1999–2008**. ([ResearchGate][14])
  * AHA and clinical guidance on “normal” RHR: updated regularly; current statements appear in **2021–2024** guideline and statistics documents. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][13])

* **Source name(s)**

  * **“Resting pulse rate reference data for children, adolescents, and adults: United States, 1999–2008”** (NHANES). ([ResearchGate][14])
  * **AHA / major clinical reviews** on resting heart rate and cardiovascular risk. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][13])
  * **Educational health resources** (e.g., VerywellHealth, Everlywell) that summarize normal RHR ranges, used only as support for the 60–100 bpm “normal” range. ([Verywell Health][15])

* **Methodological / limitation notes**

  * RHR is highly **variable between individuals and over time** (stress, medications, cardiovascular fitness, measurement position), so even a true population “average” would have limited meaning for individual assessment. ([News-Medical][16])
  * NHANES measurements were taken under standardized conditions, but the 1999–2008 timeframe precedes major secular changes (e.g., obesity trends, medication use), so using those data as a 2020+ benchmark would be misleading.

### (3) Limitations & Ethical Considerations

* **Missing / inconsistent age-specific data (30–39)**

  * For all four indicators, **no 2020+ U.S. national surveillance dataset accessible here provides clean, age- and sex-specific means for the narrow 30–39 group (or women 30–39)**.
  * Sleep and physical-activity surveillance since 2020 often report **categories (short vs recommended sleep; meeting vs not meeting guidelines)** rather than raw means, and RHR is typically framed in terms of “normal ranges” rather than national averages.

* **Pre-2020 limitations**

  * Where numeric values exist (NHANES sleep means; accelerometer steps; resting pulse reference data), they are drawn from **1999–2018** and thus classified as **pre-2020 background**. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][1])
  * Health behaviors and measurement technologies have **changed substantially** since those periods (smartphones, wearables, COVID-19 disruptions), making direct application of older averages to today’s 30–39-year-olds inappropriate. ([cdc.gov][12])

* **Population averages vs individual health**

  * Even if perfect age-specific means existed, **population averages cannot and should not be used to judge individual health**:

    * Sleep need varies by genetics, health conditions, and work schedules. ([cdc.gov][3])
    * MVPA, steps, and RHR are strongly shaped by occupation, disability, medications, and fitness level; a person can be healthy while being above or below the “average.” ([ncbi.nlm.nih.gov][17])

* **Ethical caution in usage**

  * These benchmarks are **for contextual education only**, not for diagnosis, treatment, or individualized risk stratification.
  * Applying them to individuals or small groups (e.g., by race/ethnicity, income, or workplace) risks **over-generalization and stigmatization**, especially where local environments differ markedly from national averages. ([jamanetwork.com][4])
  * Any digital coaching or AI-driven feedback built on these numbers should:

    * Emphasize **ranges and trends**, not “good vs bad” labels.
    * Encourage users to discuss health metrics with **licensed clinicians**, especially for heart rate and sleep disorders.
    * Make **data gaps explicit** (for example, clearly stating “no reliable 2020+ U.S. estimate is available for this metric in women 30–39”).
